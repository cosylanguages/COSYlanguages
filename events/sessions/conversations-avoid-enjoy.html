Psychology Today 📖

Many people find small talk exhausting yet deeply crave meaningful connections with others. This session investigates the gap between the conversations we typically have and the ones we actually enjoy. We will explore the psychological barriers to deep conversation and how to move beyond surface-level interactions in our daily lives.

📖 Session Vocabulary

Cognitive bias
a systematic error in thinking that affects how people perceive reality and make decisions.
A cognitive bias makes people assume a conversation with a stranger will be boring.
Social perception
the way we interpret and understand other people and social situations.
Our social perception of strangers is often inaccurate.
Anticipatory anxiety
anxiety that occurs before an event, based on what we expect might happen.
He felt anticipatory anxiety before starting a conversation with a stranger.
Negative expectation
believing that something will turn out badly before it happens.
A negative expectation can stop people from engaging in conversations.
Social reward
the positive emotional feeling we get from interacting with others.
A good conversation can activate a strong social reward.
Emotional forecasting
predicting how we will feel in the future.
People are often wrong in their emotional forecasting about conversations.
Perceived awkwardness
the feeling that a situation will be socially uncomfortable, even if it isn’t.
Perceived awkwardness prevents many people from talking to strangers.
Social avoidance
the tendency to avoid social interactions or situations.
Social avoidance can increase feelings of loneliness.
Interpersonal connection
a psychological sense of closeness between people.
Even short conversations can create an interpersonal connection.
Self-fulfilling prediction
a belief that causes itself to become true through behavior.
Expecting a conversation to be awkward can make it actually awkward.

🎙️ Discussion Structure

🟠 Warm-up▲
Imagine you are waiting for a bus and the person next to you looks friendly.
  • Do you start a conversation, or do you look at your phone? Why?
🔵 Round 1 — Analysis▲
Questions
Why do you think we often assume a stranger will be “not interesting” before we even speak to them? (Consider the role of Cognitive bias here.)
★ When was the last time you were surprised by someone after talking to them?
Why does a simple conversation sometimes feel like a bigger risk than it really is? (Consider the role of Social perception here.)
★ Do you ever hesitate before starting small talk?
Why do people often remember awkward moments more than good conversations? (Consider the role of Anticipatory anxiety here.)
★ Do you tend to replay social situations in your mind afterwards?
How much of our social life is shaped by negative expectation rather than real experience?
★ Do you think you miss opportunities because of expectations?
Why do we usually underestimate the social reward of casual conversations?
★ Have you ever walked away from a chat feeling unexpectedly good?
Why is emotional forecasting so unreliable when it comes to social situations?
★ Can you think of a time you avoided something that turned out fine?
What actually creates perceived awkwardness — silence, people, or our own thoughts?
★ What makes a conversation feel awkward for you personally?
Do you think social avoidance is more about personality or habit?
★ In what situations do you avoid talking to people?
How can a short chat still create a real interpersonal connection?
★ Have you ever felt “strangely connected” to a stranger?
Can a self-fulfilling prediction really shape how a conversation goes?
★ Do you think confidence changes outcomes or just feelings?
🟣 Let's Speak Together▲

Debate: Is small talk a "necessary social ritual" or a "waste of mental energy"? Use words like 'social reward' and 'perceived awkwardness' in your arguments.

🟢 Round 2 — Dilemmas / Future▲
Debate
If you were told that most strangers secretly enjoy being talked to more than you think, would you start more conversations? (How does Cognitive bias affect this statement?)
If you could see a “probability score” above people’s heads showing how likely a conversation is to go well, would you trust it? (How does Social perception affect this statement?)
If you had to choose, would you rather relive every slightly awkward conversation you avoided or every awkward conversation you actually had? (How does Anticipatory anxiety affect this statement?)
If you were permanently free from perceived awkwardness, do you think your personality would become more open? (How does Negative expectation affect this statement?)
If you had discovered that your best future friend was sitting next to you on a train today, would you have spoken to them? (How does Social reward affect this statement?)
If you were always to trust your negative expectations, would you ever be pleasantly surprised by a new interaction? (How does Emotional forecasting affect this statement?)
If your past self could tell you that most of your “social failures” were actually normal, would you be more confident today? (How does Perceived awkwardness affect this statement?)
If every conversation you avoided had been replaced with a version of yourself saying “you missed something interesting,” would you change your behavior? (How does Social avoidance affect this statement?)
If you had to live in a world where every conversation was safe but boring, how would you find excitement in your social life? (How does Interpersonal connection affect this statement?)
If you had realized sooner that confidence is an attitude rather than an outcome, would your social life have been different? (How does Self-fulfilling prediction affect this statement?)
✏️ Teacher's Note (Linguistic Corrections)▲
It makes me feeling anxious.

'Make' + object + bare infinitive (no -ing).
I'm not interested to talk.
I'm not interested in talking.
'Interested in' + gerund (-ing).
I am agree with you→I agree with you(Agree as a verb)
