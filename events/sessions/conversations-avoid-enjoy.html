📖 Session Vocabulary

Cognitive bias
A systematic error in thinking that affects how people perceive reality and make decisions.
A cognitive bias makes people assume a conversation with a stranger will be boring.
Social perception
The way we interpret and understand other people and social situations.
Our social perception of strangers is often inaccurate.
Anticipatory anxiety
Anxiety that occurs before an event, based on what we expect might happen.
He felt anticipatory anxiety before starting a conversation with a stranger.
Negative expectation
Believing that something will turn out badly before it happens.
A negative expectation can stop people from engaging in conversations.
Social reward
The positive emotional feeling we get from interacting with others.
A good conversation can activate a strong social reward.
Emotional forecasting
Predicting how we will feel in the future.
People are often wrong in their emotional forecasting about conversations.
Perceived awkwardness
The feeling that a situation will be socially uncomfortable, even if it isn’t.
Perceived awkwardness prevents many people from talking to strangers.
Social avoidance
The tendency to avoid social interactions or situations.
Social avoidance can increase feelings of loneliness.
Interpersonal connection
A psychological sense of closeness between people.
Even short conversations can create an interpersonal connection.
Self-fulfilling prediction
A belief that causes itself to become true through behavior.
Expecting a conversation to be awkward can make it actually awkward.

🎙️ Discussion Structure

🟠 Warm-up▲

Imagine you are waiting for a bus and the person next to you looks friendly. Do you start a conversation, or do you look at your phone? Why?

🔵 Round 1 — Analysis (The everyday psychology of small talk)▲
Questions
Why do you think we often assume a stranger will be “not interesting” before we even speak to them?
Personal question: When was the last time you were surprised by someone after talking to them?
Why does a simple conversation sometimes feel like a bigger risk than it really is?
Personal question: Do you ever hesitate before starting small talk?
Why do people often remember awkward moments more than good conversations?
Personal question: Do you tend to replay social situations in your mind afterwards?
How much of our social life is shaped by negative expectation rather than real experience?
Personal question: Do you think you miss opportunities because of expectations?
Why do we usually underestimate the social reward of casual conversations?
Personal question: Have you ever walked away from a chat feeling unexpectedly good?
Why is emotional forecasting so unreliable when it comes to social situations?
Personal question: Can you think of a time you avoided something that turned out fine?
What actually creates perceived awkwardness — silence, people, or our own thoughts?
Personal question: What makes a conversation feel awkward for you personally?
Do you think social avoidance is more about personality or habit?
Personal question: In what situations do you avoid talking to people?
How can a short chat still create a real interpersonal connection?
Personal question: Have you ever felt “strangely connected” to a stranger?
Can a self-fulfilling prediction really shape how a conversation goes?
Personal question: Do you think confidence changes outcomes or just feelings?
🟣 Let's Speak Together▲
Debate: Is small talk a "necessary social ritual" or a "waste of mental energy"? Use words like 'social reward' and 'perceived awkwardness' in your arguments.
🟢 Round 2 — Future Dilemmas (Why we avoid conversations we might enjoy)▲
Debate
If you were told that most strangers secretly enjoy being talked to more than you think, would you start more conversations — or would you still hesitate?
Personal question: What’s the real thing that stops you most: fear of awkwardness or lack of interest?
If you could see a “probability score” above people’s heads showing how likely a conversation is to go well, would you trust it — or ignore it?
Personal question: Do you think good conversations are predictable at all?
Would you rather relive every slightly awkward conversation you avoided… or every awkward conversation you actually had?
Personal question: Which one haunts people more — regret of action or regret of inaction?
If you were permanently free from perceived awkwardness, do you think your personality would become more open — or just more chaotic?
Personal question: What kind of “social risk-taker” do you think you would become?
If you discovered that your best future friend was sitting next to you on a train today, would that change how you behave — or would nothing really change?
Personal question: Do you treat strangers differently when you suspect something “important” might happen?
Would you rather always trust your negative expectations and avoid disappointment, or ignore them and risk being pleasantly surprised every time?
Personal question: When has your expectation about a conversation been completely wrong?
If your past self could tell you that most of your “social failures” were actually normal and forgettable, would you become more socially confident today?
Personal question: Why do we exaggerate our own awkward moments?
If every short conversation you avoided was replaced with a version of yourself saying “you missed something interesting,” would that change your behaviour — or would you still ignore it?
Personal question: Do you think people underestimate everyday interactions too much?
Would you rather live in a world where every conversation is safe but boring, or one where conversations are unpredictable but often surprisingly rewarding?
Personal question: What makes a conversation “worth it” for you personally?
If you had to choose: always risk a self-fulfilling prediction that might make conversations awkward, or never start conversations again — what would you choose?
Personal question: Do you think confidence is mostly about outcome or attitude?
✏️ Teacher's Note (Linguistic Corrections)▲
It makes me feeling anxious.
It makes me feel anxious.
'Make' + object + bare infinitive (no -ing).
I'm not interested to talk.
I'm not interested in talking.
'Interested in' + gerund (-ing).
