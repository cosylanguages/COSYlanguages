Full Article 📖

New neuroscientific evidence suggests that our brains process social information and begin forming decisions long before we are consciously aware of them. This session explores the unconscious mechanisms behind trust, cooperation, and social judgment, challenging the traditional view of free will in human interaction. We will discuss the biological roots of our social biases and how this research might redefine our understanding of leadership, empathy, and social responsibility.

📖 Session Vocabulary

Subconscious
concerning the part of the mind of which one is not fully aware but which influences actions.
Our subconscious mind often processes social cues before we notice them.
Pre-emptive
serving or intended to forestall something, especially to prevent an attack.
The brain's pre-emptive processing helps us avoid social conflict.
Cognitive bias
a systematic error in thinking that occurs when people are processing and interpreting information.
Cognitive bias can lead us to make unfair judgments about new people.
Neural firing
the electrical signal that travels along an axon when a neuron is activated.
Neural firing patterns can predict a social decision seconds before it is made.
Cooperation
the process of working together to the same end.
The study focused on the brain's role in initiating cooperation.
Intuition
the ability to understand something immediately, without the need for conscious reasoning.
Social intuition is actually the result of rapid unconscious processing.
Empathy
the ability to understand and share the feelings of another.
Empathy is a key component of effective social decision-making.
Social cue
a verbal or non-verbal hint, which can be positive or negative, that guides social interaction.
The brain is highly sensitive to even the smallest social cue.
Amygdala
a part of the brain involved with the experiencing of emotions.
The amygdala plays a central role in our immediate social reactions.
Inference
a conclusion reached on the basis of evidence and reasoning.
We often make rapid social inferences without having all the facts.
