📚 Stage 2: Vocabulary▲

❤️ Core Concepts & Feelings

Greatness
the quality of being great, distinguished, or eminent.
She achieved greatness in her musical career.
Opposite: Mediocrity
Haunting
poignant and evocative; difficult to forget.
The haunting melody of the cello lingered in the room.
Opposite: Pleasant
Surrender
give up or hand over after a struggle.
They decided to surrender their weapons and end the conflict.
Opposite: Resist
Struggle
make forceful efforts to get free of restraint.
He had to struggle to make his voice heard in the crowd.
Opposite: Ease
Release
allow or enable to escape from confinement.
The band will release their new single next Friday.
Opposite: Hold

🗣️ Life, Actions & Connection

Echo
a sound or series of sounds caused by reflection.
Her words had a strange echo in the empty hallway.
Opposite: Silence
Wilderness
an uncultivated, uninhabited, and inhospitable region.
They went hiking in the beautiful mountain wilderness.
Opposite: Civilization
Triumph
a great victory or achievement.
Her performance was a major triumph for the opera house.
Opposite: Defeat
Legacy
something left or handed down by a predecessor.
The famous artist left a lasting artistic legacy.
Opposite: Oblivion
Grace
simple elegance or refinement of movement.
She danced with incredible grace and poise.
Opposite: Clumsiness

✨ Stage 5: Language Focus▲

Language Focus: Modal Verb of Obligation (*have to*)

In the song One Of The Greats, the repetitive lyric chunk "To be one of the greats, you have to..." highlights the use of Modal Verb of Obligation (*have to*). This structure supports: Discussing terms of career greatness and legacy.

Let's Practise — Three Examples:
  • Example 1: Practice using this structure actively during the discussion rounds.
  • Example 2: Notice how this pattern is repeated to build rhythmic familiarity.
  • Example 3: Apply this specific grammatical form in your Stage 6 final speaking challenge.

✏️ Teacher's Note (Linguistic Corrections)▲
There is a big improvement of my English → There is a big improvement in my English (Use 'in' after 'improvement' to express domain.)
It was a great relief of hearing the news → It was a great relief to hear the news (Use 'relief' + infinitive 'to hear'.)
She is in the choir since three years → She has been in the choir for three years (Use present perfect + 'for' to show duration of state.)
