What is Fusion? 📖

Nuclear fusion, the process that powers the stars, has long been the "holy grail" of clean energy research. This session examines the recent scientific breakthroughs in containment and energy output that bring us closer to a world of limitless, carbon-free power. We will discuss the immense technical challenges remaining and how the successful implementation of fusion would transform global geopolitics and the fight against climate change.

📖 Session Vocabulary

Fusion
the process of joining two or more things together to form a single entity.
Nuclear fusion powers the sun and other stars.
Fission
the action of dividing or splitting something into two or more parts.
Traditional nuclear power plants use fission to generate electricity.
Plasma
an ionized gas consisting of positive ions and free electrons.
In a fusion reactor, the fuel must be heated until it becomes plasma.
Sustainability
the ability to be maintained at a certain rate or level.
Fusion is seen as a key to long-term energy sustainability.
Breakthrough
a sudden, dramatic, and important discovery or development.
Scientists recently achieved a major breakthrough in net energy gain.
Isotopes
forms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons.
Deuterium and tritium are isotopes of hydrogen used in fusion.
Magnetic confinement
using magnetic fields to contain fusion fuel in the form of a plasma.
The tokamak design uses magnetic confinement to stabilize the reaction.
Net energy gain
when the energy generated is greater than the energy required to start the reaction.
Achieving net energy gain is the primary goal of commercial fusion research.
Abundance
a very large quantity of something.
Hydrogen is found in great abundance in the Earth's oceans.
Radioactive waste
hazardous waste that contains radioactive material.
One advantage of fusion is that it produces very little long-lived radioactive waste.
