Breaking Mendel's Laws 📖

Gregor Mendel's laws of inheritance have been the foundation of genetics for over a century, but new research is revealing cases where these "unbreakable" rules are bypassed. This session explores the fascinating world of non-Mendelian inheritance, including gene drive and epigenetic factors that challenge our understanding of biological destiny. We will discuss the implications of these discoveries for medicine, agriculture, and our fundamental view of the evolutionary process.

📖 Session Vocabulary

Inheritance
the process by which genetic information is passed from parent to offspring.
Mendel studied the inheritance of traits in pea plants.
Trait
a distinguishing quality or characteristic, typically one belonging to a person.
Eye color is a common physical trait passed down through genes.
Allele
one of two or more alternative forms of a gene that arise by mutation.
Each parent contributes one allele for every gene to their child.
Mutation
the changing of the structure of a gene, resulting in a variant form.
Some mutations can lead to the development of new, beneficial traits.
Dominant
an allele that expresses its phenotype even in the presence of a recessive allele.
The gene for brown eyes is usually dominant over the gene for blue eyes.
Recessive
an allele that only expresses its phenotype when two copies are present.
Blue eyes are a recessive trait, meaning both parents must carry the gene.
Genome
the complete set of genes or genetic material present in a cell or organism.
The human genome project took over a decade to complete.
Epigenetics
the study of changes in organisms caused by modification of gene expression.
Epigenetics shows how environment can affect which genes are active.
Probability
the extent to which an event is likely to occur.
Mendel used probability to predict the traits of future plant generations.
Anomaly
something that deviates from what is standard, normal, or expected.
The breaking of Mendelian laws is considered a significant biological anomaly.
