Diwali History 📖

📖 Session Vocabulary (10 Units)

Diyas
small oil lamps made of clay, traditionally used during Diwali
Example: Lighting diyas symbolizes the victory of light over darkness.
Rangoli
an art form in which patterns are created on the floor using materials such as colored rice, flour, or flower petals
Example: Creating a beautiful rangoli at the entrance of the house welcomes guests and good luck.
Prosperity
the state of being successful, especially in material or financial terms
Example: Diwali is a time to pray for prosperity and health for the coming year.
Festivity
the celebration of something in a joyful and exuberant way
Example: The streets were filled with music and general festivity.
Firecrackers
small explosive devices used primarily to produce a large amount of noise
Example: Although popular, firecrackers are being limited in some cities due to pollution.
Hospitality
the friendly and generous reception and entertainment of guests
Example: Diwali is famous for the warm hospitality shown by families to their neighbors.
Mythology
a collection of myths, especially one belonging to a particular religious or cultural tradition
Example: According to Hindu mythology, Diwali celebrates the return of Lord Rama.
Feast
a large meal, typically a celebratory one
Example: Families gather for a grand feast featuring traditional sweets and savory dishes.
Cleanse
to make something thoroughly clean
Example: Many people cleanse and decorate their homes before the festival starts.
Symbolism
the use of symbols to represent ideas or qualities
Example: The symbolism of light in Diwali is universal across many different faiths.

🎙️ Discussion Structure

🟠 Warm-up▲
Festival of Light
  • What is the most beautiful festival you have ever seen?
  • Do you usually light candles or lamps at home?
🔵 Round 1 — Cultural Trivia (10 Questions)▲
Quiz / Discussion
1. Diwali is celebrated by several different religions, not just Hindus.
★ Are there any multi-religious festivals in your country?
2. The festival usually lasts for five days.
3. Cleaning the house is a mandatory preparation for Diwali.
4. Sweets (Mithai) are the most common gift exchanged during the festival.
5. Diwali is timed according to the lunar calendar, so the date changes every year.
6. Wearing new clothes is a tradition for many families.
7. Gambling (with small amounts) is sometimes a traditional activity during Diwali.
8. Rangoli patterns are intended to welcome Lakshmi, the goddess of wealth.
9. Diwali is also known as the "Deepavali".
10. It is the most widely celebrated festival in India and its diaspora.

🟢 Round 2 — Future of Traditions (10 Units)▲
Debate
1. Should firecrackers be banned entirely to protect the environment?
2. Has Diwali become too commercialized, like Christmas?
3. Is it possible to celebrate a religious festival meaningfully if you aren't religious?
4. Should Diwali be a public holiday in countries with large Indian populations?
5. Are digital greetings replacing the tradition of visiting neighbors?
6. Should we prioritize traditional handmade diyas over cheap plastic lights?
7. Is the concept of "cleansing the home" more psychological than physical?
8. Can "fusion" Diwali celebrations (mixing Indian and Western traditions) be authentic?
9. Why is the theme of "light winning over darkness" so popular in human culture?
10. What can other cultures learn from the Indian tradition of hospitality?
