Thrift & Envy

This session explores Barbra Streisand's theatrical performance of the vintage classic, "Second Hand Rose". Students will learn NYC theatrical slang and mid-century conversational expressions related to secondhand lifestyle, thrift, and bargain hunting. We will discuss social comparison, family hand-me-downs, and peer envy.

📖 Session Vocabulary

Secondhand
having had a previous owner; not new.
She bought a beautiful secondhand piano for her living room.
Thrift
the quality of using money and other resources carefully and not wastefully.
Her grandmother taught her the value of thrift and saving money.
Envy
a feeling of discontented or resentful longing aroused by someone else's possessions.
She watched her classmate's brand-new car with a hint of envy.
Hand-me-down
a garment or other item that has been passed on from another person.
As the youngest child, he always wore his brother's old hand-me-down clothes.
Bargain
a thing bought or offered for sale much more cheaply than is usual.
Getting this antique table for fifty dollars was a real bargain.
Luxury
the state of great comfort and extravagant living.
They spent their holiday in absolute luxury at a five-star resort.
Imitation
a thing intended to copy something else, especially an expensive product.
The cheap plastic bag was a poor imitation of the designer leather brand.
Antique
a collectible object such as a piece of furniture or work of art that has high value because of its age.
He loves to browse local shops for beautiful antique clocks.
Gossip
casual or unconstrained conversation or reports about other people.
She promised not to listen to silly workplace gossip anymore.
Pride
a feeling of deep pleasure or satisfaction derived from one's own achievements.
She took great pride in completing the marathon under four hours.

✏️ Teacher's Note (Linguistic Corrections)▲
She bought a secondhand piano with a cheap price→She bought a secondhand piano at a cheap price(Use 'at a cheap price' or 'for a cheap price'.)
He wears his brother's hand-me-downs clothes→He wears his brother's hand-me-down clothes(When used as an adjective before a noun, use the singular form 'hand-me-down'.)
I have envy of his new luxury car→I envy his new luxury car(Use the verb 'envy' directly or say 'I feel envy towards...'.)
