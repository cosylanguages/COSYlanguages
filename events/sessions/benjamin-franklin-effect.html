Intermediate (B1)

Topic

Psychology of relationships and the phenomenon of liking those we help.

Perhaps we do not help people because we like them; perhaps we like them because we have helped them. The Benjamin Franklin Effect is a fascinating psychological phenomenon where performing a favor for someone actually increases our liking for them. This counter-intuitive behavior challenges the common belief that we only help those we already admire. This session explores how cognitive dissonance and self-perception shape our social bonds and professional networks.

📖 Session Vocabulary

Favor
something helpful that you do for another person.
She asked me for a small favor.
Relationship
the way two or more people feel and behave toward each other.
Good communication improves relationships.
Trust
the belief that someone is honest and reliable.
Trust takes time to build.
Connection
a feeling of understanding or closeness with someone.
They felt a strong connection after working together.
Cooperate
to work together toward a common goal.
Successful teams cooperate well.
Attitude
the way you think or feel about something.
His attitude changed after the discussion.
Persuade
to make someone agree with an idea or action.
The advertisement persuaded many people to buy the product.
Conflict
a disagreement or argument.
The conflict lasted for several weeks.
Appreciate
to recognize the value of something or someone.
I appreciate your help.
Reciprocity
the practice of exchanging help, kindness, or benefits.
Reciprocity is important in many friendships.

🎙️ Discussion Structure

🟠 Warm-up▲
The "Benjamin Franklin Effect" says that doing a favor for someone makes you like them more.
  • Perhaps we do not help people because we like them; perhaps we like them because we have helped them. Is our affection just a story we tell ourselves to justify our actions?
  • In one word, what makes a person "likable"?
🔵 Round 1 — Deep Discussion▲
Questions
Have you ever wondered whether you liked someone because of who they were—or because you had already done them a favor?
★ Looking back, can you think of a relationship that may have started this way?
Why does our brain seem to protect our self-image by changing our attitude instead of admitting, "I helped someone I don't even like"? (Consider the role of Relationship here.)
★ Do you usually trust your first impressions, or do they change after spending time with someone?
Can doing repeated small favors slowly create emotional trust even when two people have very little in common?
★ Have you ever become close to someone who was completely different from you?
Could the Benjamin Franklin effect explain why some people stay loyal after a conflict instead of leaving? (Consider the role of Connection here.)
★ Have you ever appreciated someone more after overcoming a disagreement?
Do people become emotionally attached to whatever they invest their time, energy, or kindness in? (Consider the role of Cooperate here.)
★ What have you become attached to simply because you invested a lot in it?
Can someone persuade you to like them without saying anything—simply by creating opportunities for you to cooperate with them? (Consider the role of Attitude here.)
★ Have you ever realized someone was slowly becoming important in your life without knowing exactly why?
Is reciprocity one of the reasons people sometimes confuse gratitude with love, friendship, or loyalty? (Consider the role of Persuade here.)
★ Have you ever mistaken gratitude for a deeper connection?
Which changes people more: receiving kindness or giving it? Why? (Consider the role of Conflict here.)
★ Which has changed your own attitude more often?
Could social media be quietly using the Benjamin Franklin effect by encouraging people to constantly interact, comment, and help others? (Consider the role of Appreciate here.)
★ Do you feel more connected to people after interacting online, or is it mostly an illusion?
If our feelings can change simply because of our own actions, how much control do we really have over who we become? (Consider the role of Reciprocity here.)
★ Has understanding psychology ever changed the way you see yourself?
🟣 Let's Speak Together▲

Activity: The Favor Scale

Discuss which of these favors is most likely to create a bond between two strangers:

Borrowing a book for a week.
Asking for directions to a specific place.
Asking for a small recommendation (e.g., a good coffee shop).
Helping someone carry a heavy bag for 5 minutes.
🟢 Round 2 — Conditional Scenarios▲
Debate
If you could instantly create trust with anyone by asking them for one harmless favor, would you use that ability—or would you rather earn every relationship naturally?
If you were to discover that your closest friendship began because of the Benjamin Franklin effect rather than genuine chemistry, would that change your appreciation of the bond? (How does Relationship affect this statement?)
If you had known this psychological effect ten years ago, would any of your major life decisions or relationships have turned out differently? (How does Trust affect this statement?)
If you were forced to end every conflict by either doing your opponent a favor or asking them for one, which option would you choose for the best long-term outcome? (How does Connection affect this statement?)
If we were impossible to persuade by psychological tricks, would we still be able to form meaningful social connections as easily? (How does Cooperate affect this statement?)
If scientists proved that reciprocity was more important than compatibility, would you spend more time managing your social "favors"? (How does Attitude affect this statement?)
If you were an employer, would you hire the person who asked for help or the person who never needed any assistance? (How does Persuade affect this statement?)
If you had to become friends with an enemy, would you have a better chance of success by doing them a favor or by letting them help you? (How does Conflict affect this statement?)
If everyone had forgotten every favor you ever did for them, how would you rebuild your reputation from scratch tomorrow? (How does Appreciate affect this statement?)
If an AI chose your future partner based on moments of mutual cooperation, would you trust the algorithm more than your own intuition? (How does Reciprocity affect this statement?)
✏️ Teacher's Note (Linguistic Corrections)▲
I ask to him a favor→I ask him a favor(Ask someone something)
It depends of the situation→It depends on the situation(Depends on)
I'm more social than him→I'm more social than he is(Comparison)
He persuaded to me→He persuaded me(Direct object)
I was sympathizing with her struggles → I was empathizing with her struggles / showed deep empathy (Linguistic register: 'sympathize' suggests feeling pity, while 'empathize' implies a deeper psychological understanding and shared feelings)
