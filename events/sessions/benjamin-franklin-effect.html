Intermediate (B1)

Topic

Psychology of relationships and the phenomenon of liking those we help.

📖 Session Vocabulary (10 Units)

Favor
something helpful that you do for another person.
Example: She asked me for a small favor.
Relationship
the way two or more people feel and behave toward each other.
Example: Good communication improves relationships.
Trust
the belief that someone is honest and reliable.
Example: Trust takes time to build.
Connection
a feeling of understanding or closeness with someone.
Example: They felt a strong connection after working together.
Cooperate
to work together toward a common goal.
Example: Successful teams cooperate well.
Attitude
the way you think or feel about something.
Example: His attitude changed after the discussion.
Persuade
to make someone agree with an idea or action.
Example: The advertisement persuaded many people to buy the product.
Conflict
a disagreement or argument.
Example: The conflict lasted for several weeks.
Appreciate
to recognize the value of something or someone.
Example: I appreciate your help.
Reciprocity
the practice of exchanging help, kindness, or benefits.
Example: Reciprocity is important in many friendships.

🎙️ Discussion Structure

🟠 Warm-up▲

The "Benjamin Franklin Effect" says that doing a favor for someone makes you like them more. Have you ever noticed this in your life?

  • "He that has once done you a kindness will be more ready to do you another, than he whom you yourself have obliged." — Benjamin Franklin.
  • In one word, what makes a person "likable"?
🔵 Round 1 — The Psychology of Liking Someone (10 Questions)▲
Questions

1. Have you ever wondered whether you liked someone because of who they were—or because you had already done them a favor?

★ Looking back, can you think of a relationship that may have started this way?

2. Why does our brain seem to protect our self-image by changing our attitude instead of admitting, "I helped someone I don't even like"?

★ Do you usually trust your first impressions, or do they change after spending time with someone?

3. Can doing repeated small favors slowly create emotional trust even when two people have very little in common?

★ Have you ever become close to someone who was completely different from you?

4. Could the Benjamin Franklin effect explain why some people stay loyal after a conflict instead of leaving?

★ Have you ever appreciated someone more after overcoming a disagreement?

5. Do people become emotionally attached to whatever they invest their time, energy, or kindness in?

★ What have you become attached to simply because you invested a lot in it?

6. Can someone persuade you to like them without saying anything—simply by creating opportunities for you to cooperate with them?

★ Have you ever realized someone was slowly becoming important in your life without knowing exactly why?

7. Is reciprocity one of the reasons people sometimes confuse gratitude with love, friendship, or loyalty?

★ Have you ever mistaken gratitude for a deeper connection?

8. Which changes people more: receiving kindness or giving it? Why?

★ Which has changed your own attitude more often?

9. Could social media be quietly using the Benjamin Franklin effect by encouraging people to constantly interact, comment, and help others?

★ Do you feel more connected to people after interacting online, or is it mostly an illusion?

10. If our feelings can change simply because of our own actions, how much control do we really have over who we become?

★ Has understanding psychology ever changed the way you see yourself?

🟣 Let's Speak Together▲

Activity: The Favor Scale

Discuss which of these favors is most likely to create a bond between two strangers:

Borrowing a book for a week.
Asking for directions to a specific place.
Asking for a small recommendation (e.g., a good coffee shop).
Helping someone carry a heavy bag for 5 minutes.
🟢 Round 2 — Psychological What If...? (10 Units)▲
Debate

1. If you could instantly create trust with anyone by asking them for one harmless favor, would you use that ability—or would you rather earn every relationship naturally?

★ In what situation would you be most tempted to use it?

2. Would you rather discover that your closest friendship began because of genuine chemistry—or because the Benjamin Franklin effect quietly created a connection after dozens of tiny favors?

★ Would knowing the truth change how much you appreciate that friendship?

3. If you had known this psychological effect ten years ago, which important relationship might have turned out differently?

★ Would you have tried to change the outcome, or let life happen naturally?

4. If you were forced to end every conflict by either doing your opponent one favor or asking them to do one for you, which option would you choose?

★ Which would feel more uncomfortable?

5. Would you rather be impossible to persuade by psychological tricks—or understand every psychological trick but still be affected by them?

★ Do you think knowing psychology actually protects people?

6. If scientists proved that most long-term relationships exist mainly because of reciprocity rather than love or compatibility, would that change your attitude toward friendship and romance?

★ Would that idea disappoint you or fascinate you?

7. If you were an employer, would you rather hire someone who constantly asks colleagues for small favors or someone who never asks anyone for help?

★ Which person would you trust with an important project?

8. If you had to become friends with someone you disliked, would you rather spend a month doing them small favors or a month accepting favors from them?

★ Which do you think would change your attitude faster?

9. Imagine you wake up tomorrow and everyone has forgotten every favor you have ever done for them—but you still remember all of theirs. How do you think your relationships would change?

★ Who would probably remain close to you?

10. If an AI could guarantee a lifelong connection between two strangers by carefully choosing the perfect series of tiny favors and moments of cooperation, would you rather let it choose your next best friend—or your future partner?

★ Would you trust the algorithm, or would you rather leave both to chance?

✏️ Teacher's Note (Linguistic Corrections)▲
I ask to him a favor→I ask him a favor(Ask someone something)
It depends of the situation→It depends on the situation(Depends on)
I'm more social than him→I'm more social than he is(Comparison)
He persuaded to me→He persuaded me(Direct object)
I have been knowing him since 2 years→I have known him for 2 years(State verb + duration)
