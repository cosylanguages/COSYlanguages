📚 Stage 2: Vocabulary▲

❤️ Core Concepts & Feelings

Casualty
a person killed or injured in an accident.
There was not a single casualty in the minor incident.
Opposite: Survivor
Conflict
a serious disagreement or argument.
The story explores the conflict between duty and personal desire.
Opposite: Harmony
Scars
a mark left on the skin or within a person's mind.
The physical and mental scars of the war healed slowly.
Opposite: Smooth skin
Painful
causing physical or mental pain.
It was a painful memory that she preferred to forget.
Opposite: Painless
Aftermath
the consequences or aftereffects of a significant event.
They worked hard to rebuild the town in the aftermath of the storm.
Opposite: Preparation

🗣️ Life, Actions & Connection

Anger
a strong feeling of annoyance or displeasure.
He struggled to control his rising anger during the argument.
Opposite: Calmness
Betrayal
the action of betraying trust or a person.
The unexpected betrayal shattered their long friendship.
Opposite: Loyalty
Survival
the state of continuing to live or exist.
Survival in the desert requires careful water management.
Opposite: Death
Devastation
great destruction or damage.
The hurricane left a trail of severe devastation.
Opposite: Construction
Reconciliation
the restoration of friendly relations.
They reached a beautiful reconciliation after many years.
Opposite: Estrangement

✨ Stage 5: Language Focus▲

The Modal Verb 'Must' for Obligation and Deduction

We use 'must' to express a strong obligation or a logical deduction directly from the lyrics.

Let's Practise — Three Examples:
  • We must dare to make our own music.
  • The song is so beautiful, she must be a great artist.
  • You must listen carefully to fill in the missing gaps.

✏️ Teacher's Note (Linguistic Corrections)▲
There is a big improvement of my English → There is a big improvement in my English (Use 'in' after 'improvement' to express domain.)
It was a great relief of hearing the news → It was a great relief to hear the news (Use 'relief' + infinitive 'to hear'.)
She is in the choir since three years → She has been in the choir for three years (Use present perfect + 'for' to show duration of state.)
