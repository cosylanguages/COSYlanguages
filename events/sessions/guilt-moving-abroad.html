Perhaps our desire to explore the world is not merely about finding new opportunities, but about the painful, quiet work of discovering who we are away from the expectations of home. This session explores the silent melancholy and guilt that often accompany the immigrant experience. Together, we will gently discuss the emotional cost of choosing our own paths, and how we might reconcile our duty to those we left behind with our own personal growth.

📖 Session Vocabulary

Self-actualization
the realization or fulfillment of one's talents and potentialities.
Moving to a major global city offered her the career self-actualization she needed.
Estrangement
the state of being alienated or separated from family or culture.
Long physical distance can sometimes lead to emotional estrangement from childhood friends.
Guilt-ridden
deeply troubled by persistent feelings of guilt.
Many expats feel guilt-ridden when celebrating major life milestones away from their parents.
Nostalgia
a sentimental longing or affection for a happy period or place in the past.
Smelling traditional home-cooked food always triggers deep nostalgia for her homeland.
Alienation
the experience of being isolated or separated from a group.
Not speaking the local language can intensify a feeling of complete social alienation.
Resentment
bitter indignation at being treated unfairly or left behind.
She struggled with subconscious resentment from family members who stayed behind.
Adaptability
the quality of being able to adjust easily to new conditions.
Moving abroad requires high emotional adaptability and openness to change.
Belonging
an affinity for a place, or a feeling of acceptance in a social group.
It can take several years of living in a country to develop a true sense of belonging.
Sacrifice
an act of giving up something valued for the sake of something more important.
Leaving your native country is a massive personal sacrifice for future opportunities.
Reconcile
to find a way of making two different situations or feelings exist together.
Expats must learn how to reconcile their old cultural roots with their new lives.

🎙️ Discussion Structure

🟠 Warm-up▲
Think about how living away from home affects your personality.
  • Perhaps leaving our native streets is the first time we truly see ourselves without the mirrors of our childhood. Have you ever spent a significant period of your life living in a different city or country?
  • Some of us find comfort in the familiar, while others feel a quiet excitement in being completely unknown. How does your heart respond to a brand new, silent environment?
🔵 Round 1 — General Discussion▲
Questions
Perhaps leaving our hometown is the only path to a radical, necessary self-actualization, even if it means leaving pieces of ourselves behind. How do you define your own path of growth?
★ What is your highest personal goal that you hope to achieve this year?
Perhaps physical distance inevitably creates a quiet estrangement from the very people who raised us. How do you keep the threads of connection from fraying?
★ How often do you call or text your parents and extended family?
Perhaps we feel deeply guilt-ridden because we have been taught that loyalty to our family is more important than our own personal happiness. How do you navigate this emotional trade-off?
★ Have you ever made a major life decision that disappointed your family?
Perhaps nostalgia is a sweet, necessary self-delusion that allows us to remember the past as more beautiful than it actually was. How do you distinguish it from paralyzing homesickness?
★ What physical object or smell makes you feel most nostalgic for your childhood?
Perhaps the quiet pain of social alienation is the price we must pay for the freedom of living in a foreign land. How can we support those who feel completely invisible in our communities?
★ Have you ever entered a social gathering where you felt like an complete outsider?
Perhaps family members who stay behind harbor a quiet resentment because they feel left in a story that someone else chose to walk out of. Have you ever felt this unspoken tension?
★ Have you ever felt jealous of a friend who achieved success abroad?
Perhaps our emotional adaptability is not a passive skill, but our mind's desperate effort to keep us safe in an unfamiliar world. How does this adaptability affect your learning of a new language?
★ Do you find it easy to adapt to a sudden change of plans?
Perhaps a true sense of belonging is not a geographical place, but the internal peace of being accepted for who we are. Can we ever feel completely at home in two different worlds?
★ Where is the place in the world where you feel most comfortable and accepted?
Perhaps every significant achievement requires a quiet, painful sacrifice that only we will ever fully understand. What is the hardest trade-off you have had to make?
★ Have you ever sacrificed a hobby or a trip to focus on your study or work?
Perhaps the ultimate challenge of adulthood is learning how to reconcile our duty to care for our aging parents with our own ambition to build a life. How do you find this delicate balance?
★ Do you believe that children have a moral obligation to live near their parents?

🟢 Round 2 — Critical Statements▲
Debate
Perhaps pursuing individual self-actualization at the expense of family cohesion is ultimately a tragic act of modern selfishness. (Do we owe our lives to those who gave them to us?)
Perhaps physical separation makes a slow, emotional estrangement completely inevitable, regardless of our best intentions. (Can love survive a decade of different daily realities?)
Perhaps an immigrant's heart is destined to remain guilt-ridden, forever torn between a home they can't return to and a land they don't fully belong to. (Is exile a permanent emotional state?)
Perhaps sweet nostalgia is a comforting sedative that prevents us from fully living in and appreciating our present reality. (Does dwelling on the past ruin our future?)
Perhaps experiencing a period of complete social alienation is the only way to build a truly independent soul. (Is loneliness a necessary fire of transformation?)
Perhaps family resentment is an unavoidable symptom of personal growth that we must learn to accept with compassion. (Can we grow without hurting those who stay behind?)
Perhaps emotional adaptability is a fixed trait, and those without it are destined to struggle profoundly when transplanted. (Can a closed mind ever truly thrive abroad?)
Perhaps a deep, secure sense of belonging can only be built during childhood, and any adult belonging is merely a beautiful imitation. (Are expats always actors in their new homes?)
Perhaps no professional success is worth the quiet sacrifice of missing the final years of our parents' lives. (Are we valuing ambition over love?)
Perhaps we can never truly reconcile our desire for complete freedom with our human need for deep, binding roots. (Are we condemned to be forever restless?)
✏️ Teacher's Note (Linguistic Corrections)▲
I am living abroad since five years→I have been living abroad for five years(Duration vs starting point: Use 'for' with periods of time in the present perfect)
I feel guilt about leaving them→I feel guilty about leaving them / I feel a sense of guilt over leaving them(Adjective pattern 'feel guilty' vs noun collocation 'feel a sense of guilt over')
It was hard to adapt to the country→It was hard to adapt to living in the country / adjust to the country(Gerund pattern: 'adapt to' is followed by a gerund or noun phrase representing a practice/situation)
I am nostalgic of my childhood home→I am nostalgic about / for my childhood home(Prepositional collocation: Adjective 'nostalgic' takes 'for' or 'about')
They left behind their old lives→They abandoned / walked away from / renounced their old lives(Elevating vocabulary with sophisticated verbs of separation and transition)
