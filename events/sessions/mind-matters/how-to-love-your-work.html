Perhaps loving our work isn't about finding the perfect job, but about understanding our own emotional needs and the quiet anxieties that drive us. This session explores our emotional and psychological relationship with our careers. We will discuss the modern expectation that a job must provide absolute meaning and high status, and why looking elsewhere might lead to true career fulfillment. Join us as we examine how to find balance and satisfaction in our professional lives.

📖 Session Vocabulary

Fulfillment
satisfaction or happiness as a result of fully developing one's abilities.
Many young professionals seek creative fulfillment in their careers.
Overestimate
to estimate or value someone or something too highly.
We often overestimate how much a high-paying job will make us happy.
Vocation
a strong feeling of suitability for a particular career or occupation.
She felt a clear vocation for teaching since her teenage years.
Socioeconomic Standing
the relative social or professional standing of someone in a social hierarchy.
Modern society often conflates a person's intrinsic worth with their socioeconomic standing.
Prestige
widespread respect and admiration felt for someone or something.
He chose the corporate law firm primarily for its global prestige.
Friction
clash or conflict between people or ideas.
Unclear job roles can create unnecessary friction among team members.
Compromise
an agreement reached by each side making concessions.
Finding a good work-life balance always requires some form of compromise.
Compensate
to make up for something unwelcome or unpleasant.
A high salary cannot compensate for an toxic working environment.
Actualize
to make reality of one's deepest potential, character, or abilities.
A professional career is only one of many avenues to actualize your life's quiet purpose.
Obsession ≠ Indifference
state of being obsessed with someone or something ≠ lack of interest, concern, or sympathy.
Her passionate devotion bordered on obsession, while his attitude was one of complete indifference.

🎙️ Discussion Structure

🟠 Warm-up▲
Think about your early career expectations and dreams.
  • Perhaps loving our work isn't about finding the perfect job, but about understanding our own emotional needs. Is it possible that our obsession with professional passion is just a modern fantasy?
  • Do you believe that everyone can find a job they love, or is that a modern myth?
🔵 Round 1 — General Discussion▲
Questions
How does our definition of personal fulfillment change as we grow older?
★ What is the most fulfilling aspect of your current daily life?
Why do we often overestimate the positive impact of a career promotion on our happiness?
★ Have you ever wanted a job badly, only to find it disappointing later?
Is it dangerous to treat your career as a lifelong vocation rather than just a job?
★ Do you feel that you have found your true professional calling?
How does seeking social socioeconomic standing through work impact our mental health?
★ How much does the opinion of others influence your career choices?
What are the psychological costs of chasing corporate prestige?
★ Would you accept a lower salary in exchange for working at a highly prestigious company?
How do you handle professional friction with colleagues who have different values?
★ Have you ever had a serious disagreement with a manager at work?
In what areas of your professional life are you willing to make a compromise?
★ What is one work boundary that you will absolutely never cross?
How can companies compensate their staff for high-stress work environments beyond financial bonuses?
★ What non-financial benefits do you value most in a workplace?
Why do we believe we must actualize our identity through our labor?
★ Do you define yourself primarily by what you do for a living?
How can we break free from our cultural obsession with work and productivity?
★ Do you find it difficult to stop checking work emails during weekends?

🟢 Round 2 — Critical Statements▲
Debate
If you could guarantee a stable income but had to work in a field you felt completely indifferent about, would you accept it?
If you were offered a highly prestigious job that made you miserable, would you keep it for the sake of your family's social standing?
If society completely stopped measuring personal worth by professional success, what would you choose to do with your days?
If you had to choose between a low-paying job that you loved passionately and a high-paying job that bored you, which would you choose?
If an algorithm could perfectly match your personality to the ideal career, would you trust its choice blindly?
If you were told that you would never find your "true vocation," would that make you feel liberated or deeply discouraged?
If you could retire today with all your material needs met, would you still want to work on projects?
If your close friends and family did not approve of your career path, would you have the courage to pursue it anyway?
If professional passion were proven to be a modern myth, how would we redefine the purpose of education?
If you had to spend the rest of your life doing a job that was useful to society but deeply unexciting, would you find fulfillment in it?
✏️ Teacher's Note (Linguistic Corrections)▲
I am working as manager since three years→I have been working as a manager for three years(Present Perfect Continuous + 'for' duration)
This job allows me to fulfill my goal→This job allows me to achieve my goals / actualize my potential(Fulfill matches potential/obligations/wishes)
He is very obsessed about his socioeconomic standing→He is obsessed with his socioeconomic standing(The correct preposition is 'obsessed with')
