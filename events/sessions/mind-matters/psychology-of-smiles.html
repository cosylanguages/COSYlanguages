Perhaps a smile is not always a sign of joy, but a shield we wear to protect ourselves and others from our sadness. Smiles are more than just expressions of happiness; they are complex social tools used for communication, masking, and building rapport. This session explores the psychological depth of facial expressions and how they influence our interactions with others. We will analyze the difference between genuine and polite smiles and the cultural rules that govern their use in daily life.

📖 Session Vocabulary

Duchenne smile
a genuine smile involving both mouth and eye muscles, linked to real enjoyment.
Researchers use the Duchenne smile to distinguish real happiness from polite expressions.
To fake a smile
to deliberately show a smile without actually feeling happiness.
She had to fake a smile during the meeting even though she was tired.
Social smile
a polite or controlled smile used in social situations, not necessarily linked to real emotion.
A social smile is common when greeting strangers or customers.
Facial cues
visible facial signs that give information about emotions.
We often rely on facial cues to judge whether someone is happy or uncomfortable.
Micro-expression
a very quick, involuntary facial expression that reveals true emotion.
A micro-expression can last less than a second but still show real feelings.
Eye involvement
activation of eye muscles that signals a genuine smile.
Eye involvement is what separates a genuine smile from a fake one.
Emotional leakage
when real emotions appear despite attempts to hide them.
Even while smiling politely, emotional leakage showed he was actually stressed.
Non-verbal communication
communication without words, using facial expressions, gestures, and body language.
Smiling is a key part of non-verbal communication in every culture.
Social masking
hiding true feelings and showing socially acceptable emotions.
People often use social masking at work to appear positive.
Cultural display rules
cultural norms that control emotional expression.
Cultural display rules explain why people smile more in some countries than others.

🎙️ Discussion Structure

🟠 Warm-up▲
The Power of the Smile
  • Perhaps a smile is not always a sign of joy, but a shield we wear to protect ourselves and others from our sadness. What does a forced smile say about our fear of vulnerability?
  • In your opinion, can a smile change the atmosphere of a room?
🔵 Round 1 — Deep Discussion▲
Questions
Why do humans rely on smiling so heavily in social life, even when it has nothing to do with real emotion? (Consider the role of Duchenne smile here.)
★ Do you think your smile is more emotional or more social?
If a Duchenne smile is “real happiness” and a social smile is “performance,” how often do you think you switch between the two in a normal day? (Consider the role of To fake a smile here.)
★ Do you ever feel like your smile has an “automatic mode”?
Why is faking a smile often easier than explaining how we actually feel? (Consider the role of Social smile here.)
★ What emotions do you most often hide behind a smile?
If facial cues are so important, why do we still so often misread people? (Consider the role of Facial cues here.)
★ Have you ever completely misjudged someone based on their expression?
Why do micro-expressions tend to appear most when people are under emotional pressure? (Consider the role of Micro-expression here.)
★ Have you ever noticed a “split second” emotion on someone’s face that contradicted what they said?
What does eye involvement reveal that a normal smile can hide? (Consider the role of Eye involvement here.)
★ Do you trust eyes more than words when judging sincerity?
Why do we sometimes notice emotional leakage in others before we notice it in ourselves? (Consider the role of Emotional leakage here.)
★ Has anyone ever pointed out an emotion you were trying to hide?
If non-verbal communication is more honest than words, why do we still rely so much on speech? (Consider the role of Non-verbal communication here.)
★ In conflict situations, do you trust tone/body language or spoken arguments more?
Is social masking a necessary skill for modern life, or does it slowly disconnect us from ourselves? (Consider the role of Social masking here.)
★ In which environments do you feel you “perform” the most?
If smiling is shaped by cultural display rules, how much of your smile is actually “yours” and how much is cultural training? (Consider the role of Cultural display rules here.)
★ Have you noticed big differences in smiling behavior between cultures?

🟢 Round 2 — Conditional Scenarios▲
Debate
If you could instantly distinguish every fake smile from a real one, how would your social interactions change? (Consider the role of Duchenne smile here.)
If you had grown up in a culture where smiling was rare, how different would your personality be? (Consider the role of To fake a smile here.)
If you were never allowed to hide your feelings, what would have happened in your last meeting? (Consider the role of Social smile here.)
If people could see your exact emotional state at all times, would you feel more vulnerable? (Consider the role of Facial cues here.)
If you had realized the power of non-verbal cues years ago, would you have been more successful? (Consider the role of Micro-expression here.)
If society rewarded absolute emotional honesty, how would our service industries change? (Consider the role of Eye involvement here.)
If you had to choose between a world with no smiles and one with constant smiles, which is worse? (Consider the role of Emotional leakage here.)
If you stopped "polite smiling" for a week, how many of your interactions would become awkward? (Consider the role of Non-verbal communication here.)
If you could eliminate one form of social performance, which would it be? (Consider the role of Social masking here.)
If you had known about the "Duchenne smile" earlier, would you have trusted different people? (Consider the role of Cultural display rules here.)
✏️ Teacher's Note (Linguistic Corrections)▲
He was smile me→He was smiling at me / He smiled at me(Smile at someone)
It is a very interesting research→It is very interesting research(Uncountable research)
I am not good in reading faces→I am not good at reading faces(Good at something)
If he would smile more, he would be...→If he smiled more, he would be...(Second conditional)
I was sympathizing with her struggles → I was empathizing with her struggles / showed deep empathy (Linguistic register: 'sympathize' suggests feeling pity, while 'empathize' implies a deeper psychological understanding and shared feelings)
