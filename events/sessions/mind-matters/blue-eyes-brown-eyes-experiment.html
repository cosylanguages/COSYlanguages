Perhaps our schools are not merely halls of learning, but emotional arenas where the young seek safety in cliques and group boundaries. This session explores the psychological anatomy of discrimination, racism, and power through Jane Elliott's landmark "Blue Eyes - Brown Eyes" simulation. We will discuss how arbitrarily assigned group statuses immediately alter human behavior, group conformity, and individual performance. Join us as we examine how to recognize our own biases and build genuine empathy in our modern society.

📖 Session Vocabulary

Discrimination
the unjust or prejudicial treatment of different categories of people.
Jane Elliott designed her classroom simulation to teach young children about discrimination.
Privilege
a special right or advantage available only to a particular group.
Having extra recess time was a privilege granted only to the blue-eyed children.
Superiority
the state of believing oneself to be better or higher in status than others.
The children on the favored side quickly developed an arrogant sense of superiority.
Inferiority
the condition of being lower in status, capability, or quality than another.
The unfavored group suffered from a temporary feeling of complete academic inferiority.
In-group
an exclusive group of people with a shared identity and interest.
Humans are evolutionary primed to prioritize the safety of their own in-group.
Out-group
those people who do not belong to a specific in-group.
Propaganda always focuses on painting the out-group as untrustworthy.
Internalize
to make an attitude or belief part of one's nature by learning.
Children quickly internalize biased statements made by authority figures.
Prejudice
preconceived opinion that is not based on reason or actual experience.
Prejudice is often a result of ignorance and lack of intergroup contact.
Empathy
the ability to understand and share the feelings of another.
Experiencing the simulation helped the children develop deep empathy for others.
Conditioning
the process of training or accustoming a person to behave in a certain way.
Our social conditioning determines how we react to people from different backgrounds.

🎙️ Discussion Structure

🟠 Warm-up▲
Think about how group divisions form in daily life.
  • Perhaps our schools are not merely halls of learning, but emotional arenas where the young seek safety in cliques. Why do we divide ourselves into tribes so easily?
  • Have you ever felt discriminated against or treated differently based on your appearance or language?
🔵 Round 1 — General Discussion▲
Questions
How does systemic discrimination affect a person's cognitive performance and confidence?
★ Have you ever noticed yourself making a snap judgment about someone based on their look?
What is the most effective way to make people aware of their own social privilege?
★ What is one privilege in your life that you are incredibly grateful for?
Why did the favored children in Jane Elliott's experiment develop such a rapid sense of superiority?
★ How do you handle people who act like they are better than everyone else?
How can a feeling of social inferiority be overcome through support and education?
★ Have you ever felt out of place or unqualified at a professional event?
Why is our loyalty to our in-group so strong, even when that group is completely arbitrary?
★ Are you a passionate supporter of a sports team or local community?
How can we encourage positive communication and cooperation with the out-group?
★ How often do you interact with people from completely different political or religious views?
Why is it so difficult for adults to unlearn biases they did internalize during childhood?
★ What is one piece of advice from your parents that you have had to unlearn?
To what extent does media representation reinforce default social prejudice?
★ Do you actively seek out movies and books from diverse cultures?
Can a simulation experiment like "Blue Eyes - Brown Eyes" teach genuine, lasting empathy?
★ When was the last time you felt deep empathy for a character in a book or movie?
How does early cultural conditioning shape our concept of politeness and rude behavior?
★ What is one polite habit in your culture that foreigners might find strange?

🟢 Round 2 — Critical Statements▲
Debate
Systemic discrimination is a permanent feature of human societies and can never be fully eradicated.
Accepting that we have unconscious privilege is the first necessary step for social progress.
An arrogant belief in national superiority is the root cause of all major international wars.
A deep sense of inferiority can act as a powerful motivator for achieving massive success.
Our loyalty to our in-group will always override our commitment to universal human rights in a crisis.
We cannot build a stable democracy without demonizing some political out-group.
It is impossible for humans to live in a society without internalizing some form of class hierarchy.
Unconscious prejudice is an evolutionary defense mechanism that historically kept our ancestors safe.
Radical empathy can make people weak and easy to manipulate by malicious actors.
Our social conditioning is so strong that the concept of "free will" is completely an illusion.
✏️ Teacher's Note (Linguistic Corrections)▲
They were discriminated because their eyes→They were discriminated against because of their eyes(The verb is 'discriminate against' someone + 'because of' noun)
He behaves like he is superiority→He behaves like he is superior(Noun 'superiority' vs adjective 'superior')
I feel empathy to them→I feel empathy for them / I empathize with them(Feel empathy for someone / empathize with someone)
