How Sleep Works 📖

📖 Session Vocabulary (10 Units)

Circadian rhythm
the natural internal process that regulates the sleep-wake cycle
Example: Looking at blue light at night can disrupt your circadian rhythm.
Insomnia
habitual sleeplessness; inability to sleep
Example: Stress is one of the most common causes of temporary insomnia.
REM sleep
Rapid Eye Movement sleep; a unique phase of sleep characterized by random movement of the eyes and dreaming
Example: Most vivid dreaming occurs during the REM sleep stage.
Sleep hygiene
habits and practices that are conducive to sleeping well on a regular basis
Example: Good sleep hygiene includes keeping your bedroom cool and dark.
Cognitive function
mental processes including attention, memory, and decision-making
Example: Even one night of poor sleep can significantly impair cognitive function.
Melatonin
a hormone that your brain produces in response to darkness, helping with sleep
Example: Darkness triggers the release of melatonin in the brain.
Sleep deprivation
the condition of not having enough sleep
Example: Long-term sleep deprivation is linked to serious health problems.
Consolidation
the process by which the brain turns short-term memories into long-term ones
Example: Sleep is essential for the consolidation of information learned during the day.
Nap
a short sleep, especially during the day
Example: A 20-minute power nap can boost alertness and mood.
Drowsiness
a feeling of being sleepy and lethargic; sleepiness
Example: Excessive daytime drowsiness could be a sign of a sleep disorder.

🎙️ Discussion Structure

🟠 Warm-up▲
The Sleeping Brain
  • Are you a morning person (lark) or a night person (owl)?
  • What is the first thing you do if you can't fall asleep?
🔵 Round 1 — Analysis (10 Questions)▲
Questions
1. Why do you think the brain needs to "shut down" every night?
★ How do you feel if you get less than 6 hours of sleep?
2. What is the most common "bad habit" that affects sleep quality?
★ Do you use your phone in bed?
3. How does sleep deprivation affect your emotional stability?
★ Are you more irritable or stressed when you are tired?
4. Why is REM sleep important for creativity and learning?
★ Do you often remember your dreams?
5. How has modern technology (screens, electricity) changed human sleep patterns?
★ Do you think our ancestors slept better than we do?
6. Does your sleep schedule change significantly during the weekend?
7. What are the benefits and drawbacks of taking daytime naps?
8. How does caffeine influence your circadian rhythm?
9. Why is memory consolidation considered one of the primary functions of sleep?
10. If you could survive on only 2 hours of sleep with no side effects, would you do it?

🟢 Round 2 — Dilemmas / Future (10 Units)▲
Debate
1. Schools and offices should start at 10 AM to accommodate natural circadian rhythms.
2. Sleep is becoming a "luxury" for the wealthy in our hyper-connected world.
3. It is unethical for companies to expect employees to answer emails late at night.
4. In the future, we will use brain implants to get the benefits of 8 hours of sleep in only 1 hour.
5. Napping at work should be socially acceptable and encouraged.
6. Most people who claim they only need 5 hours of sleep are actually damaging their health.
7. Dreams are just "brain noise" and have no real meaning or function.
8. Over-relying on melatonin supplements is dangerous for natural hormone production.
9. If you could record and watch your dreams like a movie, would you want to?
10. Society should value "rest" as much as it values "work".
✏️ Discussion (Mistakes)▲
I suggest to sleep more→I suggest sleeping more(Suggest + gerund)
I have difficulty to fall asleep→I have difficulty falling asleep(Have difficulty + -ing)
I am used to wake up at 6am→I am used to waking up at 6am(Be used to + -ing)
It depends of my rhythm→It depends on my rhythm(Depends on)
I didn't slept well→I didn't sleep well(Past simple negative)
