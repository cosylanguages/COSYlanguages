Focus

Urban Living & Mobility

The car has long been a symbol of personal freedom and autonomy, yet its dominance in urban planning is increasingly being questioned. This session explores the practicalities of car ownership versus the benefits of an active, car-free lifestyle. We will analyze how our mobility choices shape our daily stress levels, financial health, and the environment of our cities.

📖 Session Vocabulary

Mobility
the ability to move or be moved freely and easily.
Owning a car provides a great sense of personal mobility.
Infrastructure
the basic physical and organizational structures (e.g. buildings, roads, power supplies) needed for the operation of a society.
Many cities are investing in bicycle infrastructure to reduce car use.
Public transport
buses, trains, and other forms of transport that are available to the public, charge set fares, and run on fixed routes.
Using public transport is often cheaper and greener than driving.
Congestion
the state of being extremely full or blocked, especially with traffic.
Traffic congestion in the city center is a major problem during rush hour.
Maintenance
the process of preserving a condition or situation or the state of being preserved (e.g. fixing a car).
The annual maintenance of a car can be very expensive.
Autonomy ≠ Dependence
the right or condition of self-government; independence ≠ the state of relying on or being controlled by someone else.
The colony fought for local autonomy to escape their absolute dependence on the mainland.
Carbon footprint
the total amount of greenhouse gases produced to directly and indirectly support human activities.
Selling my car was the fastest way to reduce my personal carbon footprint.
Commute
travel some distance between one's home and place of work on a regular basis.
My daily commute takes 45 minutes by train but over an hour by car.
Walkability
a measure of how friendly an area is to walking.
I moved to this neighborhood because of its high walkability and local shops.
Depreciation
a reduction in the value of an asset with the passage of time.
Car depreciation is a major financial downside of buying a new vehicle.

🎙️ Discussion Structure

🟠 Warm-up▲
The Driver's Seat
  • Do you have a driving license? If so, when did you get it?
  • What is the longest car journey you have ever taken?
🔵 Round 1 — Life With▲
Narrative
How does owning a car affect your sense of freedom and autonomy? (Consider the role of Mobility here.)
★ Do you enjoy the act of driving, or is it just a tool?
What are the biggest stresses associated with car ownership? (Consider the role of Infrastructure here.)
★ Have you ever had a car breakdown at a bad time?
How does the quality of local public transport influence your decision to own a car? (Consider the role of Public transport here.)
★ Are you satisfied with the transport in your city?
Why do many people view their car as an extension of their personality? (Consider the role of Congestion here.)
★ If your personality was a car, what model would it be?
How has the cost of fuel and maintenance changed your driving habits? (Consider the role of Maintenance here.)
★ Do you track your car expenses carefully?
In what ways does car-free living improve or complicate your social life? (Consider the role of Autonomy here.)
Does the "walkability" of a city change how you feel about your car? (Consider the role of Carbon footprint here.)
How did you feel when you first learned to drive? (Consider the role of Commute here.)
Why are cars still seen as a "status symbol" in many cultures? (Consider the role of Walkability here.)
Have you ever tried a car-sharing or car-pooling service? (Consider the role of Depreciation here.)

🟢 Round 2 — Life Without▲
Projections
If personal cars were banned in city centers, how would your life change? (Consider the role of Mobility here.)
★ What would be your biggest daily challenge without a car?
Without cars, would people spend more time interacting with their local community? (Consider the role of Infrastructure here.)
★ Do you think you would know your neighbors better if you walked everywhere?
If autonomous cars become standard, will we still need to learn how to drive? (Consider the role of Public transport here.)
★ Would you trust a car that has no steering wheel?
How would the design of our homes change if we didn't need garages or driveways? (Consider the role of Congestion here.)
★ What would you do with the extra space in your home?
Without the stress of driving in traffic, would humans become less aggressive? (Consider the role of Maintenance here.)
★ Have you ever experienced "road rage"?
If we had perfect public transport, would anyone still own a private vehicle? (Consider the role of Autonomy here.)
★ What is the one thing public transport can never replace for you?
How would the tourism industry change if independent road trips disappeared? (Consider the role of Carbon footprint here.)
★ What is the best place you have ever reached only by car?
Without cars, would children have more freedom to play outside? (Consider the role of Commute here.)
★ Was your childhood neighborhood safe for playing in the street?
If fuel becomes too expensive, will cars become a "luxury for the rich" again? (Consider the role of Walkability here.)
★ How would a massive increase in fuel prices change your lifestyle?
Can a society truly be "modern" without relying on cars? (Consider the role of Depreciation here.)
★ Which city do you think is the best model for a car-free future?
✏️ Teacher's Note (Linguistic Corrections)▲
I am drive a car since five years→I have been driving for five years(Present Perfect Continuous)
I prefer to drive than take the train→I prefer driving to taking the train(Prefer A to B)
It depends of the maintenance cost→It depends on the maintenance cost(Depends on)
I am not used to drive in city→I am not used to driving in the city(Be used to + -ing)
I am agree with you→I agree with you(Verb agreement)
