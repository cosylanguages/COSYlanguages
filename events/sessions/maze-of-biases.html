Human decision-making is often influenced by cognitive biases—mental shortcuts that can lead to errors in judgment. This session explores the most common types of bias and how they affect our perceptions of reality and other people. We will discuss strategies for recognizing these patterns in our own thinking to make more objective and rational choices.

📖 Session Vocabulary

Cognitive Bias
systematic pattern of deviation from rationality.
Confirmation bias is a very common cognitive bias.
Confirmation Bias
favoring information that confirms existing beliefs.
He only reads news that agrees with him because of confirmation bias.
Hindsight Bias
tendency to see events as predictable after they have happened.
"I knew it all along" is a classic sign of hindsight bias.
Anchor
to rely too heavily on the first piece of information offered.
The initial price anchors the negotiation.
Heuristic
mental shortcut that allows people to solve problems quickly.
Heuristics are useful but can lead to cognitive biases.
Rationality
the quality of being based on or in accordance with reason or logic.
Emotions can often cloud our rationality.
Inference
a conclusion reached on the basis of evidence and reasoning.
His inference was based on several observed patterns.
Correlation
a mutual relationship or connection between two or more things.
Correlation does not always imply causation.
Perception
the way in which something is regarded, understood, or interpreted.
Our perception of risk is often biased by recent events.
Dissonance
lack of agreement; inconsistency.
Cognitive dissonance occurs when we hold two conflicting beliefs.

🎙️ Discussion Structure

🟠 Warm-up▲
Spot the Bias
  • Have you ever bought something just because it was "on sale", even if you didn't need it?
  • Do you think you are more or less biased than the average person?
🔵 Round 1 — Analysis▲
Questions
Can you recall a time you were sure you were right, but later realized you only looked at one side?
★ Have you ever tried to intentionally look for information that contradicts your opinion?
Why do we often think "I knew it all along" after an event happens?
★ Can you think of a recent event where you felt hindsight bias?
How do marketers use anchoring to influence our spending?
★ Do you usually check prices from different sources before buying something expensive?
Why is it so hard to change someone's mind once they have formed an opinion?
★ Has anyone ever successfully changed your mind about a deeply held belief?
Do you think social media algorithms increase our cognitive biases?
★ Have you noticed your "feed" only showing you things you already like or believe?
What is the "halo effect" and how does it affect our judgment of people?
★ Have you ever assumed someone was smart just because they were well-dressed or attractive?
How does the "availability heuristic" affect our perception of safety?
★ Are you more afraid of things that are frequently in the news, even if they are statistically rare?
Can we ever truly "eliminate" bias, or can we only manage it?
★ What is your personal strategy for making objective decisions?
Why is "groupthink" dangerous in professional environments?
★ Have you ever stayed silent in a meeting because everyone else agreed on a bad idea?
Which bias do you think is the most dangerous in the modern world?
★ Do you think education can help people overcome these mental traps?

🟢 Round 2 — Dilemmas / Future▲
Debate
If you hadn't been influenced by confirmation bias, would you have made the same choice in your last major purchase?
If you were in charge of a large company, how would you ensure that hiring was completely unbiased?
If you had known about the "halo effect" years ago, would you have judged someone differently?
If you were to lose your ability to make mental shortcuts, how would your daily life change?
If social media didn't exist, would our political biases be less extreme?
If you had realized your own biases sooner, would your career path have been different?
If an AI were perfectly objective, would you trust it to make your medical decisions?
If we didn't have any biases, would we be able to make quick decisions in an emergency?
If you were to move to a completely different culture, which of your current biases would survive?
If you had to pick one bias to eliminate from the world, which would it be?
