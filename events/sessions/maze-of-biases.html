📖 Session Vocabulary

Cognitive Bias
systematic pattern of deviation from rationality
Example: Confirmation bias is a very common cognitive bias.
Confirmation Bias
favoring information that confirms existing beliefs
Example: He only reads news that agrees with him because of confirmation bias.
Hindsight Bias
tendency to see events as predictable after they have happened
Example: "I knew it all along" is a classic sign of hindsight bias.
Anchor
to rely too heavily on the first piece of information offered
Example: The initial price anchors the negotiation.
Heuristic
mental shortcut that allows people to solve problems quickly
Example: Heuristics are useful but can lead to cognitive biases.
Rationality
the quality of being based on or in accordance with reason or logic
Example: Emotions can often cloud our rationality.
Inference
a conclusion reached on the basis of evidence and reasoning
Example: His inference was based on several observed patterns.
Correlation
a mutual relationship or connection between two or more things
Example: Correlation does not always imply causation.
Perception
the way in which something is regarded, understood, or interpreted
Example: Our perception of risk is often biased by recent events.
Dissonance
lack of agreement; inconsistency
Example: Cognitive dissonance occurs when we hold two conflicting beliefs.

🎙️ Discussion Structure

🟠 Warm-up▲
Spot the Bias
  • Have you ever bought something just because it was "on sale", even if you didn't need it?
  • Do you think you are more or less biased than the average person?
🔵 Round 1 — Analysis▲
Questions
1. Can you recall a time you were sure you were right, but later realized you only looked at one side?
2. Why do we often think "I knew it all along" after an event happens?
3. How do marketers use anchoring to influence our spending?
4. Why is it so hard to change someone's mind once they have formed an opinion?
5. Do you think social media algorithms increase our cognitive biases?
6. What is the "halo effect" and how does it affect our judgment of people?
7. How does the "availability heuristic" affect our perception of safety?
8. Can we ever truly "eliminate" bias, or can we only manage it?
9. Why is "groupthink" dangerous in professional environments?
10. Which bias do you think is the most dangerous in the modern world?

🟢 Round 2 — Dilemmas / Future▲
Debate
1. Action Bias vs. Omission Bias: Is it worse to cause harm by acting, or by doing nothing?
2. Should people be held accountable for biases they are not aware they have?
3. Is "gut feeling" just a collection of hidden biases?
4. Should we trust AI to make decisions for us if it is proved to be less biased than humans?
5. Is it possible to have a completely objective news source?
6. Does being highly intelligent make you more or less likely to be biased?
7. Should schools have "critical thinking" classes to specifically fight cognitive bias?
8. Is optimism a form of "positive bias"? Is it always good?
9. How will AI bias affect the next generation of children?
10. If you could "delete" one bias from your brain, which would it be?
