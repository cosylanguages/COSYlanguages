Topic

Psychology of Obsessive Attraction

Perhaps what we call an intense romantic crush is actually a state of limerence—a beautiful, desperate longing for completion through another soul. This session examines the biological and psychological architecture of our most obsessive infatuations. Together, we will gently explore the difference between the fever of limerence and the quiet reality of mature love, seeking to understand the deep vulnerabilities that drive our hearts to wild obsession.

📖 Session Vocabulary

Limerence
the state of being infatuated or obsessed with another person.
He realized that his intense feelings were a case of limerence rather than genuine love.
Infatuation
an intense but short-lived passion or admiration for someone or something.
Their summer romance was a whirlwind of infatuation that faded by autumn.
Obsessive
preoccupied with something to a troubling or excessive degree.
She had an obsessive need to check her phone for messages from him.
Reciprocate
respond to (a gesture or action) by making a corresponding one.
It is painful when your deep feelings for someone are not reciprocated.
Intrusive
causing disruption or annoyance through being unwelcome or uninvited.
In a state of limerence, intrusive thoughts about the other person occur constantly.
Euphoria
a feeling or state of intense excitement and happiness.
A single positive text message can trigger a sense of pure euphoria.
Idealize
regard or represent as perfect or better than in reality.
We often idealize our partners at the beginning of a relationship, ignoring their flaws.
Vulnerability
the quality or state of being exposed to the possibility of being attacked or harmed.
Showing your true feelings requires a great deal of emotional vulnerability.
Dopamine
a compound present in the body as a neurotransmitter associated with pleasure.
Limerence is driven by a massive release of dopamine in the brain's reward system.
Rationality
the quality of being based on or in accordance with reason or logic.
Emotional intensity often clouds our rationality and leads to impulsive decisions.

🎙️ Discussion Structure

🟠 Warm-up▲
Analyze the term 'Limerence' (coined by Dorothy Tennov).
  • Perhaps "being in love" is a magnificent, creative projection of our own unmet needs onto a stranger, whereas genuinely "loving someone" is the quiet, daily, and unromantic work of accepting them for who they truly are. How do you perceive this distinction?
  • Do you remember your first major childhood or adolescent infatuation? Was it a source of quiet joy or a gentle, confusing agony?
🔵 Round 1 — Deep Discussion▲
Questions
Perhaps limerence is more of a burden than a blessing, trapping us in a cycle of fantasy that distracts us from the beauty of our actual lives. Have you ever felt this painful obsession?
Perhaps teenage infatuation is dismissed too easily by adults, ignoring the genuine depth of feeling and emotional awakening that it represents. Why do we treat young love as a mere phase?
Perhaps obsessive thoughts about another person are a sign that we are avoiding some quiet emptiness in our own lives. How do you find balance when a crush occupies all of your focus?
Perhaps the greatest pain in relationships is when our deepest feelings are not reciprocated. Is it truly possible to remain close friends when romantic longing remains one-sided? (Consider the role of Reciprocate here.)
Perhaps we are haunted by intrusive memories of past lovers because we are longing not for them, but for the version of ourselves we were when we were with them. How do you gently lay these ghosts to rest?
Perhaps the initial euphoria of a new connection is biologically designed to fade, so that we can transition from wild excitement to stable companionate love. How do you handle this transition?
Perhaps we idealize strangers because we do not know them well enough to see their flaws, allowing our imaginations to draw a perfect portrait. Why are we so reluctant to see people as they truly are?
Perhaps extreme emotional vulnerability is the only way to build a real relationship, even though it exposes us to the possibility of devastating heartbreak. Is the risk always worth the reward?
Perhaps what we call "soulmate connection" is actually just a dopamine addiction, a chemical high that we mistake for fate. How does this scientific perspective change your view of romance?
Perhaps rationality is completely powerless in the face of romantic obsession. Have you ever successfully used logic to calm a wild heart?

🟢 Round 2 — Conditional Scenarios▲
Hypotheticals
Perhaps if limerence could be cured with a simple pill, we would lose one of the most painful yet exquisite sources of human art, poetry, and passion. (Would you choose a life of quiet calm over intense emotional highs?)
Perhaps if infatuation never faded, human society would collapse because we would be too distracted by our partners to build anything else. (Is the cooling of passion necessary for civilization?)
Perhaps if we became as obsessive about our self-knowledge as we are about our romantic partners, we would achieve true emotional maturity. (Are we using romance as an escape from ourselves?)
Perhaps if someone finally reciprocated our feelings after years of waiting, we would feel a quiet disappointment because the real person can never compete with our perfect fantasy. (Is unrequited love sometimes safer?)
Perhaps our most intrusive romantic fantasies are actually a cry for a parent's approval or a childhood security we never received. (Are we searching for our pasts in our future partners?)
Perhaps the modern pursuit of constant euphoria prevents us from developing the patience required to build a deep, slow, and lasting love. (Has our digital culture ruined our ability to commit?)
Perhaps when we idealize a partner, we are committing a gentle act of violence against them, refusing to love them for who they actually are. (Is idealization the enemy of true intimacy?)
Perhaps we fear vulnerability because we have built a society that equates sensitivity with weakness, rather than a profound courage. (How can we cultivate a gentler strength?)
Perhaps understanding our dopamine cravings is the only way to free ourselves from toxic relationship patterns that we mistake for "destiny." (Is self-awareness the ultimate cure for heartache?)
Perhaps if rationality governed all of our romantic choices, we would never take the wild, beautiful risk of loving anyone at all. (Do we need a little madness to find happiness?)
✏️ Teacher's Note (Linguistic Corrections)▲
I am in love of her→I am in love with her(Prepositional collocation with 'in love')
She makes me feeling crazy→She makes me feel crazy(Causative syntax: 'make + object + bare infinitive')
I was having obsessive thoughts on him→I was plagued by intrusive thoughts about him / preoccupied with thoughts of him(Elevating register: Use 'plagued by' or 'preoccupied with' + correct preposition 'about' or 'of')
He didn't reciprocate back my feelings→He didn't reciprocate my feelings / return my affection(Redundancy: 'Reciprocate' inherently means to give back, so 'back' is redundant. Or elevate with 'return my affection')
I am agree with your analysis→I concur with / endorse your analysis(Elevating vocabulary from incorrect auxiliary to sophisticated verbs of agreement: 'concur' or 'endorse')
