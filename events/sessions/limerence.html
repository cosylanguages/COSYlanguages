Topic

Psychology of Obsessive Attraction

Limerence is a state of mind resulting from romantic attraction to another person, typically including obsessive thoughts and fantasies. This session examines the biological and psychological mechanisms behind this intense "crush" state. We will discuss the difference between limerence and long-term love, and how to navigate the overwhelming emotions it brings.

📖 Session Vocabulary

Limerence
the state of being infatuated or obsessed with another person.
He realized that his intense feelings were a case of limerence rather than genuine love.
Infatuation
an intense but short-lived passion or admiration for someone or something.
Their summer romance was a whirlwind of infatuation that faded by autumn.
Obsessive
preoccupied with something to a troubling or excessive degree.
She had an obsessive need to check her phone for messages from him.
Reciprocate
respond to (a gesture or action) by making a corresponding one.
It is painful when your deep feelings for someone are not reciprocated.
Intrusive
causing disruption or annoyance through being unwelcome or uninvited.
In a state of limerence, intrusive thoughts about the other person occur constantly.
Euphoria
a feeling or state of intense excitement and happiness.
A single positive text message can trigger a sense of pure euphoria.
Idealize
regard or represent as perfect or better than in reality.
We often idealize our partners at the beginning of a relationship, ignoring their flaws.
Vulnerability
the quality or state of being exposed to the possibility of being attacked or harmed.
Showing your true feelings requires a great deal of emotional vulnerability.
Dopamine
a compound present in the body as a neurotransmitter associated with pleasure.
Limerence is driven by a massive release of dopamine in the brain's reward system.
Rationality
the quality of being based on or in accordance with reason or logic.
Emotional intensity often clouds our rationality and leads to impulsive decisions.

🎙️ Discussion Structure

🟠 Warm-up▲
Analyze the term 'Limerence' (coined by Dorothy Tennov).
  • What is the difference between "loving someone" and "being in love"?
  • Can you remember your very first major crush?
🔵 Round 1 — Deep Discussion▲
Questions
Have you ever experienced limerence that felt more like a burden than a blessing?
Why is teenage infatuation often dismissed by adults as "just a phase"?
In what ways can obsessive thoughts about a person interfere with your daily work and life?
Is it possible to maintain a healthy friendship if your romantic feelings are not reciprocated? (Consider the role of Reciprocate here.)
How do you deal with intrusive memories of an old flame when you are trying to move on?
Why does the initial euphoria of a new relationship always seem to fade over time?
Why do we tend to idealize people we barely know instead of seeing them as they truly are?
Do you believe that extreme vulnerability is necessary for a relationship to be successful?
How much of what we call "love" is actually just a dopamine addiction in the brain?
Can rationality ever win a fight against strong romantic emotions?

🟢 Round 2 — Conditional Scenarios▲
Hypotheticals
If limerence could be cured with a simple pill, would you take it to save yourself from heartache?
If infatuation never faded, would humans ever be able to focus on anything other than their partners?
If you became obsessive about your career instead of a person, would you be more successful?
If a person finally reciprocated your feelings after years of waiting, would the reality live up to your fantasy? (How would Reciprocate change this situation?)
If intrusive thoughts could be projected on a screen for everyone to see, would you be embarrassed?
If you could trigger euphoria at will, would you still seek out romantic connections?
If you didn't idealize your partner, would you still be attracted to them?
If vulnerability was seen as a weakness by everyone, how would our society change?
If dopamine was the only reason we stayed together, would long-term marriage still exist?
If rationality ruled all romantic decisions, would the world be a better place?
