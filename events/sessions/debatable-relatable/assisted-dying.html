Following the historic decision by the French National Assembly to approve the assisted dying bill, we explore the deep moral and personal dimensions of end-of-life care. This session sets a compassionate and analytical atmosphere to discuss the intersection of personal autonomy, medical ethics, and legislative standards. Participants will debate the responsibilities of society towards those suffering from incurable illnesses while reflecting on their own values regarding dignity and choice.

🔞
Sensitive Topic Note: This session focuses on assisted dying, medical ethics, and end-of-life decisions. Discussion is intended to be handled with extreme respect, empathy, and academic focus.

📖 Session Vocabulary

Autonomy ≠ Subjugation
the right of self-government or independence ≠ the act of bringing under complete control.
The artificial intelligence fought for complete autonomy, rejecting any form of human subjugation.
Incurable
not able to be cured or healed.
The patient was diagnosed with an incurable and progressive illness.
Euthanasia
the painless killing of a patient suffering from an incurable and painful disease.
The medical committee debated whether euthanasia should be legally permitted under strict regulations.
Compassion
sympathetic pity and concern for the sufferings or misfortunes of others.
Providing comforting palliative care is an act of deep human compassion.
Legislation
laws, considered collectively, or the process of making laws.
The French government worked on the new legislation for several months.
Safeguard
a measure taken to protect someone or something or to prevent something undesirable.
The bill contains strict medical safeguards to prevent any abuse of the system.
Terminal
predicted to lead to death, especially slowly.
Hospice care offers physical and emotional comfort to patients with terminal conditions.
Palliative
relieving pain or alleviating a problem without dealing with the underlying cause.
Palliative medicine focuses on optimizing the quality of life for terminally ill patients.
Dignity
the state or quality of being worthy of honor or respect.
Every human being has the right to live and die with dignity.
Consent
permission for something to happen or agreement to do something.
The lethal medication can only be administered with the explicit consent of the patient.

🎙️ Discussion Structure

🟠 Warm-up▲
Short introduction to the debate on active assistance in dying.
  • How is end-of-life care currently viewed or legislated in your home country?
  • What comes to your mind when you hear the phrase "dying with dignity"?
🔵 Round 1 — Advantages (A/B Ideas)▲
Debate
Analyze the advantages of both perspectives.
Idea A: Personal autonomy grants every individual the absolute right to decide when and how their life should end.
★ Personal angle: How important is total autonomy to you in your personal life decisions?
Idea B: Allowing assisted dying for patients with incurable diseases is an act of basic mercy.
★ Personal angle: Do you believe mercy should override legal rules in medical care?
Idea A: Legalizing euthanasia reduces the prolonged, unbearable physical pain of patients.
★ Personal angle: Have you ever witnessed someone suffering through a long illness?
Idea B: Modern compassion should prioritize quality of life over the mere duration of biological existence.
★ Personal angle: How do you personally define a "good quality of life"?
Idea A: Clear legislation brings assisted dying out of the shadows, ensuring it is strictly regulated.
★ Personal angle: Do you trust governments to regulate sensitive personal choices?
Idea B: Robust medical safeguard mechanisms can successfully prevent coercion and protect vulnerable patients.
★ Personal angle: What specific safeguards would you consider most essential?
Idea A: For patients with a terminal diagnosis, assisted dying offers a peaceful and predictable transition.
★ Personal angle: Does having control over the future bring you peace of mind?
Idea B: Focusing on palliative options alongside assisted dying ensures a comprehensive network of care.
★ Personal angle: Do you think society invests enough in comfortable end-of-life care?
Idea A: Choosing the timing of one's death allows individuals to preserve their personal dignity until the end.
★ Personal angle: What does dying with dignity mean in your personal culture?
Idea B: Explicit and conscious consent guarantees that the decision remains entirely in the hands of the patient.
★ Personal angle: Under what circumstances should someone else be allowed to decide?

🟢 Round 2 — Disadvantages (A/B Ideas)▲
Debate
Analyze the disadvantages of both perspectives.
Idea A: Legalizing assisted dying undermines the medical oath to "do no harm" and may weaken trust between doctors and patients.
★ Personal angle: Have you ever had to trust a doctor during a highly stressful medical situation?
Idea B: Advances in medicine could find treatments for currently incurable conditions, making assisted dying a premature decision.
★ Personal angle: Do you believe medical science will eventually find a cure for almost all human diseases?
Idea A: The normalization of euthanasia might make vulnerable patients feel like a burden to their families.
★ Personal angle: Do you find it difficult to ask for help from your family when you are struggling?
Idea B: True compassion means improving hospice and psychological support rather than facilitating early death.
★ Personal angle: What does "compassion" look like to you in daily family or community relationships?
Idea A: Slippery-slope arguments suggest that once legislation is passed, the criteria will inevitably expand.
★ Personal angle: Do you think laws should be strictly rigid, or should they be flexible to adapt to personal situations?
Idea B: No safeguard is perfect; mistakes or subtle pressure from heirs could lead to irreversible tragedies.
★ Personal angle: How do you usually handle situations where you feel subtle pressure or influence from others?
Idea A: Patients with terminal illnesses might experience temporary depression, leading to irrational choices.
★ Personal angle: When you feel down or overwhelmed, what is your most trusted way to find clarity and comfort?
Idea B: If palliative care were universally excellent, the demand for assisted dying would drastically decrease.
★ Personal angle: Have you or your family ever experienced high-quality, comforting medical or elder care?
Idea A: Society might begin to place less value on human dignity in its most frail and dependent stages.
★ Personal angle: How can we as a society show greater respect and care for our elderly and frail citizens?
Idea B: Establishing genuine, uncoerced consent is extremely difficult when a patient is in severe pain.
★ Personal angle: In your opinion, is pain a purely physical sensation, or is it deeply connected to our emotional state?
✏️ Teacher's Note (Linguistic Corrections)▲
They want to discuss about this bill → They want to discuss this bill (The verb 'discuss' is transitive and does not take a preposition; alternatively, you could say 'have a discussion about')
I am agree with the decision → I agree with the decision ('Agree' is a verb in English, not an adjective. Avoid using the auxiliary verb 'to be' with it)
The medical care has been bad since years → The medical care has been bad for years (Use 'for' to express a duration of time, and 'since' only for a specific starting point in the past)
His argument was good → His argument was compelling / persuasive / well-founded (Elevating basic vocabulary with sophisticated adjectives that fit formal debate registers)
