Domestic Life & Resilience

This session analyzes Carly Simon's soft-rock hit, "Coming Around Again". We will focus on household vocabulary, domestic life, and the predictable cycles of everyday routines. Students will discuss relationship struggles, personal breakdown, and the resilience needed to rebuild connection and comfort.

📖 Session Vocabulary

Domestic
relating to the running of a home or to family relations.
They divided the domestic chores equally to keep the house clean.
Resilience
the capacity to recover quickly from difficulties; toughness.
She showed amazing resilience after her business failed during the crisis.
Routine
a sequence of actions regularly followed.
His morning routine always began with a hot cup of black coffee.
Breakdown
a mechanical failure, or a sudden collapse in mental health.
The sudden breakdown of their car left them stranded on the highway.
Optimism
hopefulness and confidence about the future.
His natural optimism kept everyone cheerful during the storm.
Cycle
a series of events that are regularly repeated in the same order.
The natural cycle of seasons brings different challenges every year.
Predictable
behaving or occurring in a way that is expected.
The plot of the romance movie was highly predictable but still enjoyable.
Struggle
make forceful efforts to get free of restraint or constriction.
The small company had to struggle to survive the economic downturn.
Connection
a relationship in which a person or thing is linked with something else.
Traveling helped her build a deep connection with nature.
Comfort
a state of physical ease and freedom from pain or constraint.
She found great comfort in reading her favorite childhood books.

★ Do you have enough free time?

✏️ Teacher's Note (Linguistic Corrections)▲
She has a lot of domestic chores to do→She has many domestic chores to do(Use 'many' or 'a lot of' for countable nouns. While 'a lot of' is correct, 'many' sounds cleaner in professional discussions.)
His routine is predictable than mine→His routine is more predictable than mine(Always use 'more' before multi-syllable adjectives when forming comparatives.)
She struggled for complete the project→She struggled to complete the project(Use 'struggle to' followed by an infinitive verb.)
